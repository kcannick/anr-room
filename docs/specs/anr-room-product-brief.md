# The A&R Room — Product Brief & Strategy

*Source-of-truth document for the A&R Room as a standalone product. A&R Room is its own entity. Hitmail is a separate, later bet that a successful A&R Room would justify pursuing — it appears in this document only as a downstream horizon, never as a dependency. Everything here is either live today, a near-term build, or a named horizon, and the distinction is called out throughout.*

*Operator: Makin' It Magazine (makinitmag.com). Status: live weekly product; series layer pre-build.*

---

## 1. What this is

The A&R Room is a weekly live music-review broadcast with a participation layer that turns the audience from spectators into competitors.

Every Wednesday (7–11 PM), records are submitted, played, and given live feedback by a host and co-host, multi-streamed to six platforms at once. Artists pay for placement and exposure; that is the existing business. The new layer lets **viewers tune in and rate each record**, earning points for how well they read the room. Points accrue across a tagged set of weekly sessions — a monthly **Series** — onto a dedicated leaderboard. At month's end, the top 4–8 scorers are invited to **A&R Wars**, a monthly online head-to-head tournament for a cash prize.

The product is two competitions running in one room: artists compete to make the chart, the audience competes to read the room. The show is the arena where the two meet, and the host/co-host expert feedback is the credibility layer that neither a pure-crowd nor a pure-clout review can match.

---

## 2. Market diagnosis — the squeezed middle

The music-review market has lost legibility. It has sorted into two extremes, and the middle — where real discovery actually happens — is being hollowed out.

**The bottom floods the field with volume.** An influx of hobbyists with an $80 livestream setup do free or $5–20 reviews. They aren't better, but there are so many of them that the category loses the ability to distinguish signal from noise. Price and quality stop being legible.

**The top sells borrowed clout into the resulting confusion.** Established names, former executives, and platforms like Nero monetize the *appearance* of access — $100+ submissions that function as a high-priced lottery, where most have no real interest in helping the artist. They aren't delivering more; they're *signaling* more, and they exploit a market that can no longer read value any other way.

**The serious artist, unable to read the market, chases the names.** A recognizable artist or former exec is the only legible proxy for "this person can do something for me." So serious money flows past the middle toward the clout — not because the clout delivers, but because it's the only signal the artist can find.

**The middle is squeezed from both directions for the same underlying reason: the market can't signal who actually delivers.** The middle (DJs, promoters, platforms that genuinely convert reviews into discovery and have real pathways) delivers the most and signals the worst, because "we have a real audience and real pathways" is harder to convey than a blue check or a famous name.

This is the wedge.

---

## 3. The wedge — participation as a legibility machine

The A&R Room makes the middle's real asset — a live, engaged audience with real pathways — **visible, participatory, and competitive.**

When an artist submits to a clout-merchant, there's a host and a void. When an artist submits to the A&R Room, there's a host, a co-host, *and a live audience actively rating the record and competing to read it right.* The artist doesn't have to take "we have a real audience" on faith — they can see the room rate, see the leaderboard, see the stakes. The participation layer is proof the room is real. It replaces borrowed clout (a name's reputation) with demonstrated reality (a visible, participating room).

**Why this is defensible:** the participation layer is only worth building for an operator who is telling the truth about having a room. A hobbyist can't conjure a real audience to compete. A clout-merchant *won't* give the crowd a real voice, because their model depends on the artist not seeing that the room is empty or that the feedback is a lottery. Giving the crowd a visible, competitive stake is a costly signal — expensive or pointless to fake — which is exactly what makes it credible.

**The honest limit:** the moat is the room and the operating quality, not the code. Anyone can build a poll. A larger middle-tier competitor with an existing audience could copy the mechanic. The defensibility is a head-start on a format that stays defensible only while it's executed better than anyone who notices — through the specific community, the real pathways (A&R Wars, the discovery funnel), and the quality of the feedback. This is a position to be operated, not a patent to be held.

*Note on framing: the legibility/costly-signal analysis in this section is the internal "why this wins." It is not the artist-facing pitch. Artists care that real people will hear their song and that there's a real path forward — keep the market theory in the strategy doc and out of the marketing.*

## 4. The two-sided competitive engine

The core engine is a flywheel where each side's competition makes the other side more valuable:

The **audience** competes — free — for a cash prize by listening and reading the room well → a competitive, engaged audience makes the room demonstrably real → a demonstrably real room is what an **artist** is actually paying for → artist placement revenue funds the prize and the operation → the prize and the promo pull more audience → repeat.

The viewers' competition is not a cost center run for engagement's sake. **It is the thing that makes the artist's dollar worth spending.** Audience attention has been turned into a product sold to artists; artist money has been turned into a prize that buys more audience attention. Both sides are "in the competition": the artist competes to make the chart, the audience competes to reach A&R Wars.

This is the symmetry the product is built around — competitive on both sides — and it is what no extreme can replicate, because the clout end has no audience to activate and the hobbyist end has no pathway to compete for.

## 5. The product loop

The weekly cycle, end to end:

1. **Submissions open.** Two paths feed the room (see §7 for revenue detail):
   - **Free lottery** — any registered user can submit at no cost into a pool; 10 are randomly selected for play, *but you must be live in the room when called to be played.* This is the audience cold-start engine: it converts submission desire directly into live attendance, filling the room from the artists' own followings, and refers free submitters toward joining as an A&R for a shot at the cash prize.
   - **Service Pack** (paid placement) — guaranteed inclusion and exposure, tiered.
2. **The broadcast.** Wednesday 7–11 PM, six-platform multistream. Records are played; host and co-host give live feedback.
3. **Live rating.** Viewers rate each record and predict the room's average, scoring on how well they read the room (skill, exponential-falloff scoring — the existing 0–9 mechanic).
4. **Series accrual.** Each Wednesday session is tagged into the current monthly Series. Points roll up onto the Series leaderboard.
5. **The monthly cut.** At series close, the top 4–8 on the Series leaderboard are invited to A&R Wars.
6. **A&R Wars** — a monthly online head-to-head tournament (Sunday cadence), multi-competitor, every-man-for-himself, run on the binary-poll "Verzuz" mechanic. One winner takes the cash prize.

The records competed over in A&R Wars are drawn from the Service Pack pool — these are the placements everyone must choose from. (See §8 for the integrity wall that keeps this clean.)

---

## 6. The growth engine

Three compounding growth mechanisms sit on top of the loop:

**Sanctioned bonus rounds (the network play — the largest lever).** Other reviewers' sessions can be sanctioned as *official bonus rounds* in your Series. Their session is tagged into your Series → their audience must **register on your platform** to earn points and reach the leaderboard → those registrations become **your contacts** → and the sanctioned reviewer gets to market the cash prize to their audience *without funding it themselves.* You trade prize-marketing rights (cheap — the prize exists regardless of how many sessions feed it) for audience registration and contact acquisition (valuable). Every registered contact is a future customer for Service Pack, MiMbership, and artist services. The participation layer acquires the contact; the revenue products monetize it. This is the multi-tenant thesis: become the participation infrastructure the squeezed middle runs on.

**Weekly social carousel.** A single recurring post-show graphic doing four funnel jobs at once: (1) **top 8 songs from the review** — artist-facing proof and bait, sells the next submission; (2) **current A&R leaderboard top 8** — audience-facing status and FOMO, screenshot-and-repost fuel, sells the next tune-in; (3) **A&R Wars promo + "become an A&R, win cash" CTA** — converts spectators into competitors; (4) **"submit your music to make next week's chart" CTA** — the revenue ask, reframed as earning a spot in a real competition rather than buying an opinion. The show generates next week's promo automatically. (Build these by hand first; auto-generate from app data only once the format is locked.)

**Artist testimonial content.** Collab/testimonial posts from the artist with the #1 song of the week — credibility content that delivers on the pathway promise and pulls the next wave of submissions.

## 7. Revenue

Four lines, in order of current weight:

**Service Pack (the engine).** The consolidated rebrand of the record-pool / USB pool / "Break Your Record" placement product. Three tiers, roughly $50 / $100 / $150, sold as **exposure and distribution**: inclusion in the monthly pool sent to DJs and tastemakers, guaranteed livestream play and viewer rating at higher tiers, contact/visibility to VIP guests, promo graphics and collab posts. These are the records that populate the A&R Wars pool. *(Live pricing on the current sales page runs a sale band of $49/$99/$149 off $99/$199/$349 list; the standing three-tier intent is the ~$50/$100/$150 structure.)*

**Weekly review submissions.** Standard / priority / VIP paid review slots, capped per show (≈10 of each type) to protect feedback quality, plus the limitless free lottery (10 selected). The caps are deliberate scarcity, not a limitation to engineer away.

**MiMbership + artist services.** The existing membership and à la carte artist services (marketing/PR, playlisting, magazine, etc.) — monetizing the contact base the participation layer grows.

**A&R Wars sponsorship (the new line the online pivot unlocks).** Moving A&R Wars from a two-person physical exhibition at the monthly mixer to a *recurring monthly online tournament* turns it into a sponsorable property: "A&R Wars, presented by [brand]," with a leaderboard, qualifiers, and a Sunday finale. A predictable, branded, audience-attached monthly season is a sellable container in a way a one-off exhibition never was. Sponsor-funded prizes can launch as product/experience (lighter compliance) and graduate to larger cash once papered (see §8, §11).

## 8. Integrity architecture

The integrity wall is not legal hygiene bolted on top — it is the thing that makes the room sellable at all. A leaderboard that can be bought is worth nothing to anyone, and the moment artist money can buy a score, the A&R Room becomes the clout-tier it differentiates against. The honesty *is* the product on both sides.

**Exposure, never scores.** Paid placement (Service Pack) buys a record *exposure and activity* — a spot in front of the room — never a good rating and never a tournament win. The current sales page already enforces this framing explicitly ("The A&R Room is for feedback. The Record Pool is for exposure and activity"). Keep it there. The host/co-host feedback and the room's ratings stay honest regardless of what an artist paid.

**Free-entry, skill-only on the audience side.** The audience competition that awards the cash prize is free to enter and ranked purely on skill (reading the room). No payment is required to qualify, compete, or win; no spending can affect rank. This is the precedented free-entry skill-contest structure the operator has run cleanly before.

**The two revenues stay walled.** Artist placement money and audience point-earning never touch. Artists pay to *enter the chart* (be seen), not to *win* it; viewers qualify on *skill*, not spend. The wall between the two competitions is what keeps both leaderboards credible.

**Sanctioned-session standard.** Because sanctioned bonus rounds feed the Series leaderboard that determines A&R Wars qualifiers, sanctioned partners must run to a defined standard (honest, server-scored sessions) so a sloppy or rigged partner session can't inject noise into qualification. The scoring is server-computed and skill-based, which holds most of this automatically; the *operational* standard is what makes "official bonus round" mean something.

*This section is product reasoning, not legal advice. The cash-prize and sponsorship items in §11 warrant counsel before the relevant scale.*

## 9. The series layer (the one new build)

Everything downstream of weekly rating already exists or is specced. The connective tissue — the **Series** — is the single genuinely new build.

**Design principle: the Series is a display-metadata container, not a controller.** Title, description, and (optionally) a target session count and/or date range are *for end-user display only* — they describe intent. They do **not** determine which sessions count. Membership is determined by an explicit **tag**: a session is assigned to a series in the admin UI. This is what preserves flexibility for off-schedule bonus streams — a special Friday session counts toward the series if and only if you tag it in. Letting a date range auto-include sessions would destroy exactly that flexibility, so dates and counts stay labels, never filters.

**Series object:**
- `id`, `title`, `description`, `status` (upcoming / active / closed)
- `target_sessions` (optional, display only)
- `start_date` / `end_date` (optional, display only)
- `created_at`

**Session → series link:** a `series_id` column on `sessions` (a session belongs to at most one series). The simpler one-series-per-session model is almost certainly right; multi-series membership via a join table is a clean later superset if ever needed — do not build it preemptively.

**Series points — live-computed, not stored.** Series leaderboard points are computed on the fly by summing vote points across the series' tagged sessions:

```sql
SELECT v.user_id, SUM(v.points) AS series_points
FROM votes v
JOIN rounds r   ON v.round_id = r.id
JOIN sessions s ON r.session_id = s.id
WHERE s.series_id = ?
GROUP BY v.user_id
ORDER BY series_points DESC
LIMIT 8;
```

Live-compute is correct here and stays correct through retroactive tagging, re-ratification, and vote corrections — the board is always right by construction, with no rollup to drift out of sync. Cost scales with vote rows *in one series* (≈ a month of sessions), not the whole database: at event scale (tens of songs × a room of voters × ~4–6 sessions ≈ low tens of thousands of rows), Postgres sums this in single-digit milliseconds.

**Efficiency guards (in order of when you'd reach for them):**
1. **Index the join path** — `sessions.series_id`, `rounds.session_id`, `votes.round_id`. Add the `series_id` index when adding the column.
2. **Cache the computed leaderboard for 5–10 seconds** *only if* live-read frequency during a broadcast ever spikes (a large concurrent audience all loading a live board). This is a read-frequency problem, not a data-size problem, and the fix is a short cache, not a schema change.
3. **Denormalize never** — a stored `series_points` column you increment on every vote would have to stay in sync through ratification, re-ratification, corrections, and retroactive tagging. That trades guaranteed correctness for a performance fix you don't need. Same discipline as the heavy-migration rule: don't pay complexity cost ahead of need.

**The qualification cut** is `ORDER BY series_points DESC LIMIT N` on a series at close — trivial once the leaderboard exists.

**Monthly reset model.** "Monthly tournament" falls out of the design for free: a new month = a new Series object (new container, fresh leaderboard), last month's stays intact as history, this month's Wednesdays get tagged into the new one. Clean monthly seasons with no special reset logic.

## 10. Build sequence

The calendar sequences the build naturally:

1. **Series layer — now.** Schema migration (`series` table + `series_id` on `sessions` + index), server endpoints (create/edit series, tag session, series leaderboard, qualification cut), admin tagging UI, leaderboard view. *(Mockup-first for the UI per standing preference.)* This runs every Wednesday immediately and starts the qualification race accruing.

2. **Binary-poll "Verzuz" build — during the first qualification month.** Per the existing `binary-poll-build-spec.md` (prototype validated, build order written). Needed by the time the first monthly online A&R Wars runs — roughly 3–4 weeks after the series layer ships, which is when the first month's top 4–8 are determined. The series-layer runway covers this build.

3. **First online A&R Wars — Sunday, end of first series month.** The qualified top scorers compete on the binary mechanic for the cash prize.

Sanctioned bonus rounds ride on the multi-tenant architecture + the §8 sanctioning standard, layered in once the single-operator loop is proven.

## 11. Risks & open questions

**The legal pass (the one item to close before scale).** The structure is clean — free-entry, skill-only audience competition; Service Pack buys exposure, not wins. But the A&R Wars pivot stacks four variables that each nudge toward scrutiny and together form the profile that warrants counsel: a **bigger cash prize**, **monthly cadence**, **online reach** (crosses state lines freely), and **sponsor money** in the prize. Two specific things to put to a lawyer: (a) the **Service-Pack-records-feed-a-cash-prize-tournament** seam — paid placements populate the pool the prize is contested over; the cleanest framing (already on the live page) is that the Service Pack buys *promo distribution* and tournament inclusion is a consequence, not the purchase, but "cleanest framing" is the phrase that means *confirm it*; and (b) the **sponsored cash-prize mechanics** — official rules, state registration/bonding thresholds, winner 1099s above $600, jurisdiction exclusions. Not a blocker and not urgent-scary: do it *before the first sponsored monthly Wars,* and launch interim prizes as product/experience (lighter compliance) until the cash structure is papered. *(Not legal advice; this names what to ask, not the answer.)*

**Capacity ceiling — your attention is the product and it doesn't scale.** Up to ~40 records (30 paid + 10 lottery) plus Service Pack records in one ~4-hour weekly session, with real feedback on each, is a hard ceiling, and expert feedback is the first casualty of volume. The per-type caps already manage this with scarcity (which is on-brand — "only N spots a week"). The deeper implication: *you can't scale you, so you scale the system* — which is precisely why the sanctioned-bonus-round network matters. The capacity ceiling and the network play are the same insight from two directions.

**Sanctioned-session integrity.** Covered by the §8 standard, but flagged as an ongoing operational dependency: a sanctioned partner's room behavior affects who qualifies for your tournament. The standard is what protects the qualification's credibility as the network grows.

**Two-front competition.** You compete for *audience attention* against all Wednesday-night entertainment, and for *artist dollars* against other reviewers. These need different proof and different messaging; the carousel + testimonial content serve both, but the two value props shouldn't be blurred into one.

**Prize / unit economics — make it a decision, not an accident.** Decide whether the prize is a deliberate audience-acquisition subsidy or pot-funded, and whether it's fixed or scales with submissions (a growing, visible pot can itself be a promo lever and aligns incentives). Service Pack is the real revenue engine, so the show likely funds itself comfortably — but state the model rather than discovering it.

**Multi-account edge case.** In a winner-take-all, every-man-for-himself format, *collusion has no rational payoff* (helping a rival hurts your own rank; only one wins). The residual is one person farming multiple accounts to occupy several leaderboard slots and improve their odds of a Wars seat — an identity/abuse problem, minor at current scale, worth a light guard, not a system.

**Platform-surface dependency (mitigated).** The room lives on third-party livestream surfaces, but six-platform simultaneous multistream means no single platform can deplatform you out of existence. You own the app and the data (the durable assets); you diversify the rented attention surface. Managed, not eliminated.

---

## 12. The horizon

A successful A&R Room — a proven participation loop, a real competitive audience, a sanctioned-bonus-round network turning the squeezed middle's reviewers into a contact-and-customer engine — is what would justify pursuing **Hitmail** (the separate "fantasy football for music" daily-prediction product) as the next, larger bet. The A&R Room earns that right by proving the core thesis cheaply and live: that people will rate, predict, compete, and return. Hitmail is not a dependency of this document and not a parent of this product. It is the next thing this one, if it works, makes worth building.

The whole bet here reduces to one question, answerable in the room every Wednesday: **does a real, competitive, participating audience make the middle's review worth more than the clout it's losing to?** Build the series layer, run the loop, read the answer off the leaderboard.
